Automating Quality Assurance in Radiotherapy Clinical Trials

Role: Graduate ResearcherWhere: LPC Caen & BaclesseSupervisor: Prof. Juliette ThariatStack: Python, PyTorch, Pydicom

Radiotherapy QA tells you a plan failed, not why. I built a geometric descriptor of anatomical complexity so QA can tell a badly-planned case apart from an intrinsically hard one — the foundation of my PhD.

The problem

In radiotherapy clinical trials, every plan is checked by experts before delivery — quality assurance. Today that check is largely manual and returns a blunt verdict: pass or fail. When a plan fails, it doesn’t say why.

A plan can fail because it was planned badly, or because the patient’s anatomy is so complex that no plan could fully spare the organs at risk. Treating both the same makes QA inconsistent across centres and hard to standardise. We need a way to measure how intrinsically hard a case is.

What is Tumour Topology Complexity?

Tumour Topology Complexity (TTC) quantifies how intrinsically difficult a tumour is to treat, based on the anatomical proximity of organs to the target and what dose gradients a machine can actually deliver. I capture it with a descriptor I formulated: the Geometric Volume Coefficient (GVC).

From the distance-to-target histogram

For each organ at risk I start from its differential distance-to-target histogram (dDTH) — how much organ volume sits at each distance from the target — and split it into three zones:

  • Overlap — volume inside the target (intrinsic complexity).
  • Near-compression — too close to spare given the machine’s dose-gradient limit.
  • Safe zone — far enough to be spared.
The dDTH split into three zones. The overlap and near-compression fractions drive the GVC, normalised to 0–1: a higher value means a harder organ to spare.

From organ to patient

Organ GVCs are aggregated into a single patient score with a quadratic, self-weighted formulation (GVC²×w): high-complexity organs dominate without low ones being ignored, and each organ carries a clinical-priority weight drawn from QUANTEC/RTOG standards rather than chosen arbitrarily — so the result is standardised across centres.

Integrating it into QA

With the descriptor in hand, the QA verdict can be made fairer. If both target coverage and OAR sparing are met, the plan passes; if neither is met, it fails. But when only one goal is met, the TTC grade decides the interpretation:

  • True — both goals met.
  • False — neither goal met.
  • Suboptimal — one goal missed at low TTC: a genuine planning fault.
  • TTC-limited — one goal missed at high TTC: anatomy-limited, the best achievable.
The proposed four-class QA. The complexity grade separates a true planning fault from an anatomy-limited case — information a binary pass/fail discards.

Where this is heading

Next is automation: predicting the four classes directly from data — starting with attention-based Multiple Instance Learning on tabular DVH data, then patient DICOM, then a multimodal fusion on the REACH database.

Status: descriptor formulated and validated on real patient data; integration logic defined; multimodal automation in progress.
