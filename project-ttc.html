Master's thesis · current

Automating Quality Assurance in Radiotherapy Clinical Trials

Role: Graduate ResearcherWhere: LPC Caen & BaclesseSupervisor: Prof. Juliette ThariatStack: Python, PyTorch, Pydicom

Radiotherapy QA tells you a plan failed, not why. I built a geometric descriptor of anatomical complexity so QA can tell a badly-planned case apart from an intrinsically hard one — the foundation of my PhD.

The problem

In radiotherapy clinical trials, every plan is checked by experts before delivery — quality assurance. Today that check is largely manual and returns a blunt verdict: pass or fail. When a plan fails, it doesn’t say why.

A plan can fail because it was planned badly, or because the patient’s anatomy is so complex that no plan could fully spare the organs at risk. Treating both the same makes QA inconsistent across centres and hard to standardise.

My approach — Quantify, Integrate, Automate

The thesis introduces a way to measure how hard a case actually is, integrate it into the QA verdict, and ultimately learn it automatically.

The proposed four-class QA: a failed plan is split by its complexity grade into a true planning fault (Suboptimal) or an anatomy-limited case (TTC-limited).

What I have done so far

A geometric descriptor: the Geometric Volume Coefficient (GVC)

From each organ’s differential distance-to-target histogram (dDTH), I split the organ into three zones: overlapped volume (intrinsic complexity), near-compression volume that can’t be spared due to machine dose-gradient limits, and safe volume. These combine into a normalised GVC on a 0–1 scale.

The dDTH split into three zones. The overlapped and near-compression fractions drive the GVC; a higher value means a harder organ to spare.

From organ to patient

Organ GVCs are aggregated with a quadratic, self-weighted formulation (GVC²×w): high-complexity organs dominate without low ones being ignored, each weighted by clinical priority from QUANTEC/RTOG standards.

Where this is heading

Next is automation: predicting the four classes directly from data — starting with attention-based Multiple Instance Learning on tabular DVH data, then patient DICOM, then a multimodal fusion on the REACH database.

Status: descriptor formulated and validated on real patient data; integration logic defined; multimodal automation in progress.
