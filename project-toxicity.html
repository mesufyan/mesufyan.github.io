Research internship

Toxicity Prediction in Head & Neck Cancer using Machine Learning

Role: Research InternWhere: LPC CaenSupervisors: Prof. Juliette Thariat, Ozgur OzerStack: Python, LightGBM, Optuna, SHAP

A reproducible LightGBM pipeline that predicts xerostomia, mucositis and dysphagia in 684 patients from clinical and dose-volume data — with interpretability built in and limitations stated plainly.

★ View repository

The problem

Modern radiotherapy delivers remarkably conformal dose, yet patients still develop normal-tissue toxicities that classical low-dimensional models struggle to predict. For head & neck cancer this is acute: targets sit among organs that control salivation, swallowing and speech, so small dose differences translate into real quality-of-life effects.

The question I tackled: can routinely collected clinical variables and DVH-derived dose descriptors predict, patient by patient, the risk of three key toxicities — xerostomia, mucositis, and dysphagia?

What I did

I built an end-to-end, reproducible machine-learning pipeline on a multicentre cohort of 684 head & neck patients from the GORTEC trial network, treating each toxicity as a binary outcome (CTCAE grade 3–5 = severe).

  • Data engineering: linked baseline, treatment, toxicity and follow-up tables; ordinal-encoded clinical stage; engineered BMI and dose-per-fraction; ran automated integrity checks (deepchecks).
  • DVH features: extracted dose metrics for anatomically relevant organs at risk per endpoint — parotids and submandibular glands for xerostomia, oral cavity for mucositis, PCM and oesophagus for dysphagia.
  • Modelling: LightGBM gradient-boosted trees, with an 80/20 stratified split and stratified 3-fold CV used only inside the training set; Optuna hyperparameter search; KNN imputation for DVH features.
  • Interpretability: SHAP and feature-importance analysis to check the learned drivers were clinically plausible.

Results

Enriched DVH-“bag” features improved discrimination for two of the three endpoints. Crucially, the most important features were clinically sensible — parotid dose dominated xerostomia, oral-cavity dose dominated mucositis.

Performance stayed deliberately honest: DVHs compress a full 3D dose field into a few numbers, so they can only go so far. That finding directly motivates the spatial, multimodal direction of my thesis.

What I took from it

The pipeline is reproducible and the limitations are explicit — modest cohort size, single test split, and the information lost when 3D dose is reduced to DVH summaries. The natural next step, which I am now pursuing, is richer dose/anatomy representations and multiple-instance learning that keeps spatial structure.

Code, preprocessing and model pipeline are public on GitHub.
